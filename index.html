01 Biological Bases of Behavior

Understanding how the nervous system, brain, and neurotransmitters drive behavior is the foundation of biological psychology. Every thought, emotion, and action traces back to neural activity.

The Neuron

Neurons are the basic signaling cells of the nervous system. A typical neuron has dendrites that receive signals, a cell body (soma) containing the nucleus, an axon that transmits the signal, and terminal buttons (synaptic knobs) that release neurotransmitters across the synapse. Many axons are wrapped in a myelin sheath, a fatty insulation produced by Schwann cells (PNS) or oligodendrocytes (CNS), which speeds conduction via saltatory conduction at the Nodes of Ranvier.

Action Potential

Neurons fire according to the all-or-none principle. At rest, a neuron sits at approximately -70 mV (resting potential). When stimulation reaches threshold (about -55 mV), voltage-gated sodium channels open and the cell depolarizes rapidly to about +30 mV. Then potassium channels open, causing repolarization. A brief hyperpolarization follows before the neuron returns to resting potential. The refractory period prevents immediate re-firing and ensures signals travel in one direction.

Resting Potential: ~-70 mV → Threshold: ~-55 mV → Depolarization: ~+30 mV → Repolarization → Hyperpolarization → Rest

Tracing an Action Potential Step by Step

A sensory neuron in your fingertip detects heat. Here is what happens inside that neuron:

Step 1 — Resting StateThe neuron sits at −70 mV. The inside of the cell is more negative than the outside. Sodium (Na+) wants to rush in, but channels are closed.
Step 2 — Threshold ReachedThe heat stimulus depolarizes the membrane to about −55 mV. This is the threshold. It is all-or-none: either the neuron fires fully or not at all.
Step 3 — DepolarizationVoltage-gated Na+ channels fly open. Sodium floods in. The inside shoots to about +30 mV. This is the peak of the action potential.
Step 4 — RepolarizationNa+ channels close. K+ channels open. Potassium rushes out, bringing voltage back down.
Step 5 — HyperpolarizationK+ channels close a little late, dipping the voltage briefly below −70 mV. During the refractory period the neuron cannot fire again immediately.
Step 6 — ResetThe sodium-potassium pump restores balance. The neuron is ready to fire again.

Exam tip: Remember that depolarization = Na+ IN and repolarization = K+ OUT.

Why Parkinson's and Schizophrenia Seem Like Opposites

Both disorders involve dopamine, but in opposite directions. This is a great exam pattern to know.

Parkinson's DiseaseNeurons in the substantia nigra that produce dopamine die off. Result: too little dopamine in motor circuits. Symptoms = tremor, rigidity, slow movement. Treatment: give the brain a dopamine precursor (L-DOPA).
Schizophrenia (positive symptoms)Excess dopamine activity in certain brain pathways. Result: hallucinations and delusions. Treatment: dopamine-blocking antipsychotics.
The PatternDopamine too low = movement problems. Dopamine too high = thought problems. This is why antipsychotic drugs sometimes cause Parkinson-like movement side effects — they block too much dopamine.

The Nervous System

The nervous system divides into the Central Nervous System (CNS = brain + spinal cord) and the Peripheral Nervous System (PNS). The PNS further splits into the somatic nervous system (voluntary muscle control) and the autonomic nervous system (involuntary functions). The autonomic system has two branches: the sympathetic nervous system ("fight or flight," which increases heart rate, dilates pupils, and inhibits digestion) and the parasympathetic nervous system ("rest and digest," which slows heart rate and promotes digestion).

Lateralization: The left hemisphere generally handles language, logic, and analytical tasks. The right hemisphere specializes in spatial processing, face recognition, and emotional expression. Split-brain studies by Sperry and Gazzaniga demonstrated this after corpus callosum severing.

Endocrine System Highlights

The endocrine system uses hormones (chemical messengers in the bloodstream) that work more slowly but have longer-lasting effects than neurotransmitters. The pituitary gland is the "master gland," controlled by the hypothalamus. The adrenal glands produce cortisol (stress hormone) and epinephrine/norepinephrine (adrenaline). The thyroid regulates metabolism. The pineal gland produces melatonin for sleep-wake cycles.

02 Sensation & Perception

Sensation is the process of detecting physical energy from the environment and encoding it as neural signals. Perception is the brain's interpretation and organization of those signals into meaningful experience.

Vision

Light enters the eye through the cornea, passes through the pupil (controlled by the iris), and is focused by the lens onto the retina. The retina contains photoreceptors: rods (peripheral vision, dim light, black-and-white) and cones (central vision, color, detail, concentrated in the fovea). Visual information travels via the optic nerve, crosses at the optic chiasm, passes through the lateral geniculate nucleus (LGN) of the thalamus, and reaches the primary visual cortex in the occipital lobe.

Pathway: Cornea → Pupil → Lens → Retina (rods/cones) → Bipolar cells → Ganglion cells → Optic nerve → Optic chiasm → LGN (thalamus) → Visual cortex

Color vision theories: the trichromatic theory (Young-Helmholtz) says three types of cones (red, green, blue) combine to produce all colors. The opponent-process theory (Hering) says color is processed in opposing pairs (red-green, blue-yellow, black-white). Both theories are correct at different stages of processing.

Why You See an Afterimage: Opponent-Process in Action

Stare at a red square for 30 seconds, then look at a white wall. You see a green afterimage. Here is why.

During StaringThe red-sensitive opponent cells are firing intensely and begin to fatigue.
After Looking AwayThe fatigued red cells go quiet. Their opponent partner — the green side — is now unopposed and fires strongly.
What You PerceiveThe dominant green signal is interpreted as a green afterimage on the white wall.
Why Trichromatic Theory Alone Cannot Explain ThisThe trichromatic theory describes the three cone types in the retina, but afterimages arise from opponent processing further along the visual pathway in the LGN. You need both theories together.

Hearing

Sound waves enter the ear canal, vibrate the tympanic membrane (eardrum), which transmits vibrations through the ossicles (hammer, anvil, stirrup) to the oval window. This creates waves in the cochlear fluid that bend hair cells on the basilar membrane, converting mechanical energy to neural signals. These travel via the auditory nerve to the auditory cortex in the temporal lobe.

Place theory explains perception of high-frequency sounds (different locations on the basilar membrane). Frequency theory explains low-frequency sounds (neurons fire at the same rate as the sound wave). The volley principle extends frequency theory to moderate frequencies.

Depth Perception

Monocular cues (one eye): relative size, interposition/overlap, linear perspective, texture gradient, aerial perspective, and motion parallax. Binocular cues (two eyes): retinal disparity (each eye gets a slightly different image) and convergence (eyes turn inward for near objects).

Key Concept
Top-down vs. Bottom-up processing: Bottom-up processing builds perception from raw sensory data (data-driven). Top-down processing uses expectations, knowledge, and context to interpret stimuli (conceptually-driven). Most perception involves both simultaneously.

03 Learning & Conditioning

Learning is a relatively permanent change in behavior due to experience. The three major types are classical conditioning, operant conditioning, and observational learning.

Classical Conditioning (Pavlov)

An organism learns to associate two stimuli so that one comes to elicit a response originally caused only by the other. Before conditioning, the unconditioned stimulus (US) naturally produces the unconditioned response (UR). A neutral stimulus (NS) produces no relevant response. After repeated pairing of the NS with the US, the NS becomes a conditioned stimulus (CS) that produces a conditioned response (CR).

Before: US (food) → UR (salivation) | NS (bell) → no response
During: NS (bell) + US (food) → UR (salivation)
After: CS (bell) → CR (salivation)

Classical Conditioning in Real Life: Fear of Dentists

Many people develop a fear of dentists they did not have as small children. Here is exactly how that happens using conditioning terms.

Before ConditioningThe dental drill (NS) means nothing emotionally. Pain (US) naturally causes fear/anxiety (UR).
Conditioning TrialAt the dentist, the drill sound (NS) is repeatedly paired with pain (US), producing fear (UR).
After ConditioningNow the drill sound alone (CS) triggers fear and anxiety (CR) even before any pain occurs. The patient feels anxious just hearing the drill spin up.
GeneralizationThe fear generalizes: now the smell of the office, the white coat, even the building itself triggers anxiety.
Treatment via ExtinctionA therapist might use systematic desensitization: gradual, repeated exposure to drill sounds without pain until the CR weakens and disappears.
Classical conditioning terms and definitions
TermDefinition

Operant Conditioning (Skinner)

Behavior is shaped by its consequences. Reinforcement increases the likelihood of a behavior; punishment decreases it. "Positive" means adding a stimulus; "negative" means removing one.

Types of operant conditioning with examples
TypeActionEffect on BehaviorExample
Positive reinforcementAdd pleasant stimulusIncreasesGiving a treat for good grades
Negative reinforcementRemove aversive stimulusIncreasesSeatbelt buzzer stops when buckled
Positive punishmentAdd aversive stimulusDecreasesSpanking after misbehavior
Negative punishmentRemove pleasant stimulusDecreasesTaking away phone privileges
Mnemonic
Think of "positive" as adding (+) and "negative" as subtracting (-). "Reinforcement" always strengthens behavior. "Punishment" always weakens behavior.

Negative Reinforcement vs. Punishment: The Confusion Ends Here

The word "negative" makes students assume this is bad or painful. It is not. Negative means removal.

Negative Reinforcement ExampleYou have a pounding headache (unpleasant stimulus). You take ibuprofen and the headache goes away. Because removing the pain followed taking the pill, you are more likely to take ibuprofen next time. Behavior (taking pill) INCREASED because something bad was REMOVED. That is negative reinforcement.
Positive Punishment ExampleA student talks in class (behavior). Teacher assigns extra homework (add unpleasant thing). Student talks less. Behavior DECREASED because something bad was ADDED. That is positive punishment.
The Quick TestAsk two questions: (1) Did the behavior increase or decrease? Increase = reinforcement, decrease = punishment. (2) Was something added or removed? Added = positive, removed = negative. You cannot get it wrong with this method.

Observational Learning (Bandura)

Learning by watching others (models). Bandura's Bobo doll experiment showed children imitate aggressive behavior they observe. Four steps: attention (notice the model), retention (remember what was observed), reproduction (ability to replicate), and motivation (reason to perform). This introduced the concept of vicarious reinforcement, where seeing a model rewarded increases imitation.

Key Concept
Learned helplessness (Seligman): When organisms learn that they cannot control outcomes, they stop trying even when escape becomes possible. This has been linked to depression and passivity.

Memory is the persistence of learning over time through encoding, storage, and retrieval. Models of memory explain how information moves from fleeting sensory input to lasting knowledge.

Why Cramming Fails: The Memory Model in Action

You study for four hours the night before an exam and feel confident. Three days later, most of it is gone. Here is the memory science behind why.

What Cramming DoesInformation moves from sensory memory into short-term memory through attention. But massed practice (cramming) relies on maintenance rehearsal — just repeating things over and over. This keeps information in STM but does not strongly encode it into long-term memory.
The Encoding ProblemLong-term memory consolidation requires elaborative rehearsal — connecting new information to what you already know. Cramming rarely achieves this depth of processing (Craik and Lockhart).
The Spacing EffectMultiple shorter sessions spread over days are dramatically more effective. Each session reactivates the memory trace, strengthening the long-term storage pathway.
The FixStudy for 45 minutes today, review for 20 minutes tomorrow, and do a brief recall test in three days. You will retain far more than four straight hours the night before.

Types of Long-Term Memory

Explicit (declarative) memory involves conscious recall and divides into episodic memory (personal events and experiences) and semantic memory (general knowledge and facts). Implicit (nondeclarative) memory operates without conscious awareness and includes procedural memory (motor skills and habits), classical conditioning effects, and priming.

Mnemonic
Explicit = "Expressed" consciously: Episodic (events) + Semantic (stuff you know). Implicit = "Implied" automatically: Procedural (skills) + Priming + Conditioning.

Availability Heuristic: Why You Fear Plane Crashes More Than Car Crashes

Flying is statistically far safer than driving, yet many people fear it more. The availability heuristic explains why.

The Heuristic at WorkWhen you judge how dangerous flying is, your brain asks: "How easily can I picture a plane crash?" Plane crashes dominate news coverage, are dramatic, and are easy to vividly imagine. Car accidents, though far more deadly in total, are routine and receive little media attention.
The ErrorEase of recall gets mistaken for probability. Because plane crashes are vivid and memorable, the brain inflates their likelihood. Because car crashes are forgettable, the brain deflates their likelihood.
The CorrectionLook at base rates (actual statistics). Per mile traveled, flying is about 50 times safer than driving in the US. Logic overrides the heuristic when you consult real data.

Language

Language has structure at multiple levels: phonemes (smallest sound units), morphemes (smallest meaningful units), syntax (grammatical rules), and semantics (meaning). Noam Chomsky proposed the language acquisition device (LAD), an innate biological capacity for language, and distinguished between surface structure (how a sentence is worded) and deep structure (its underlying meaning). The critical period for language acquisition is roughly before puberty.

Key Concept
Linguistic relativity hypothesis (Sapir-Whorf): Language influences thought. The strong version claims language determines thought (largely rejected). The weak version claims language influences perception and categorization of experience (widely accepted).

Intelligence

Spearman proposed general intelligence (g factor). Gardner proposed multiple intelligences (linguistic, logical-mathematical, spatial, musical, bodily-kinesthetic, interpersonal, intrapersonal, naturalistic). Sternberg's triarchic theory includes analytical, creative, and practical intelligence. Emotional intelligence (Salovey and Mayer, popularized by Goleman) involves perceiving, understanding, managing, and using emotions.

IQ tests: the Stanford-Binet and Wechsler scales are most common. A reliable test gives consistent scores; a valid test measures what it claims to measure. IQ scores follow a normal distribution with mean 100 and standard deviation 15.

Four Theories of Emotion: Same Event, Four Different Stories

You are walking alone at night and hear footsteps behind you. How do the four theories explain what you feel?

James-LangeYour heart pounds and you start to sweat (arousal first). You notice these body changes and interpret them as fear. "I'm trembling, therefore I must be afraid."
Cannon-BardYour heart pounds AND you feel fear at exactly the same time, independently. The brain sends signals to both your body and your cortex simultaneously.
Schachter-Singer (Two-Factor)Your heart pounds (arousal). You look around for a context clue — dark alley, strange footsteps. You label this arousal "fear." If you were at a concert, the same pounding heart might be labeled "excitement."
Lazarus (Cognitive Appraisal)Before anything else, your brain quickly appraises: "Is this a threat?" That split-second cognitive judgment triggers both the emotion and the body response. Thought comes first.
Key Concept
Yerkes-Dodson Law: Performance increases with arousal up to a point, then declines. Simple tasks benefit from higher arousal; complex tasks perform best at moderate arousal levels.

Conservation: The Classic Juice Cup Test

A child watches you pour juice from a short wide glass into a tall thin glass. You ask: "Is there more juice now, the same, or less?" The answer depends on their Piagetian stage.

Preoperational (ages 2-7): Fails the TaskThe child says "more juice!" because the tall glass looks fuller. This is centration — the child focuses only on the height dimension and ignores width. They do not yet understand that quantity is conserved when appearance changes.
Concrete Operational (ages 7-11): Passes the TaskThe child says "same juice" and can explain why: "you just poured it, nothing was added or removed." They have mastered conservation and can apply reversibility (it could be poured back).
Why This Matters for ExamsThe key word is "conservation" = understanding that quantity stays constant despite changes in appearance. Mastery occurs at the start of the concrete operational stage, around age 7.

Attachment (Ainsworth)

Using the Strange Situation experiment, Ainsworth identified attachment styles. Secure attachment (about 60%) involves a child who explores freely with the caregiver present, shows distress at separation, and is comforted upon return. Insecure-avoidant children show little distress and avoid the caregiver. Insecure-anxious/ambivalent children are clingy, very distressed at separation, and not easily comforted. Disorganized attachment shows inconsistent, confused behavior. Harlow's monkey studies demonstrated that contact comfort (soft cloth mother) was preferred over nourishment (wire mother with food), showing attachment is more than feeding.

Key Concept
Vygotsky's Zone of Proximal Development (ZPD): The gap between what a learner can do alone and what they can do with guidance. Scaffolding provides support that is gradually removed as the learner becomes competent.

08 Personality

Personality is the characteristic pattern of thinking, feeling, and behaving that makes each person unique. Major perspectives offer different explanations for how personality develops and functions.

Psychoanalytic/Psychodynamic (Freud)

Freud proposed three structures of personality: the id (pleasure principle; unconscious, impulsive desires), the ego (reality principle; mediates between id and superego), and the superego (morality principle; internalized societal standards and conscience). Personality develops through psychosexual stages: oral (0-1), anal (1-3), phallic (3-6, includes the Oedipus/Electra complex), latency (6-puberty), and genital (puberty onward). Fixation at any stage can affect adult personality.

Spotting Defense Mechanisms in a Scenario

A student fails an important exam. Watch how several defense mechanisms could appear in their response.

Denial"I didn't fail. The professor must have made a grading mistake." Refuses to acknowledge the reality of the poor grade.
Rationalization"That class was a waste of time anyway. It's not even relevant to my major." Creates a logical-sounding reason to feel okay about the failure.
Projection"The other students must have cheated. Nobody could pass that exam honestly." Transfers the student's own sense of inadequacy onto others.
DisplacementGoes home and snaps at their roommate. Redirects frustration to a safer target than the professor.
SublimationChannels the frustration into intense studying and earns the highest grade in the class on the next exam. This is considered the healthiest defense mechanism.

Humanistic Perspective

Carl Rogers emphasized unconditional positive regard, the need for congruence between the real self and ideal self, and a person-centered approach. Maslow's hierarchy of needs culminates in self-actualization. This perspective focuses on free will, personal growth, and subjective experience.

Trait Perspective

The Big Five (OCEAN) model is the most widely accepted trait framework. Traits are relatively stable dispositions that describe how people typically think, feel, and behave.

Mnemonic
OCEAN: Openness (curiosity, creativity), Conscientiousness (organization, dependability), Extraversion (sociability, energy), Agreeableness (cooperation, trust), Neuroticism (emotional instability, anxiety)

Social-Cognitive Perspective (Bandura)

Reciprocal determinism describes the interaction among behavior, personal factors (thoughts, feelings), and environmental factors. Self-efficacy is one's belief in their ability to succeed at a task, and it strongly predicts behavior and outcomes. An internal locus of control (belief that you control your fate) is associated with greater achievement and well-being than an external locus of control (belief that outside forces control your fate).

09 Psychological Disorders

Schizophrenia neurotransmitter hypothesis: The dopamine hypothesis suggests that overactivity of dopamine is associated with positive symptoms. Newer research also implicates glutamate dysfunction. Structural changes include enlarged ventricles and reduced cortical volume.

Positive vs. Negative Symptoms of Schizophrenia: A Scenario

The words "positive" and "negative" do not mean good and bad. They mean present/added versus absent/reduced. Here is a patient scenario to make this concrete.

Positive Symptoms (Things Added)The patient hears voices commenting on their actions (auditory hallucinations). They believe the government has implanted a chip in their brain (delusion of persecution). Their speech jumps between unrelated ideas (disorganized thinking). These are called "positive" because they are things present that should not be there.
Negative Symptoms (Things Removed)The patient speaks in a flat, emotionless tone (flat affect). They stop going out, stop calling friends, and stay in bed most of the day (social withdrawal). They have no interest in activities they used to enjoy (anhedonia). These are called "negative" because they are things absent that should be there.
Treatment ImplicationAntipsychotic drugs (dopamine blockers) treat positive symptoms fairly well. Negative symptoms are harder to treat and often persist, significantly affecting quality of life.

10 Social Psychology

Social psychology studies how individuals' thoughts, feelings, and behaviors are influenced by the actual, imagined, or implied presence of others.

Attribution Theory

We explain others' behavior using dispositional attributions (internal traits) or situational attributions (external circumstances). The fundamental attribution error (FAE) is our tendency to overattribute others' behavior to disposition while underestimating situational factors. The self-serving bias leads us to attribute our successes to internal factors and failures to external factors.

The Fundamental Attribution Error in a Campus Scenario

You are walking across campus and a fellow student bumps into you and does not apologize. What do you think?

The FAE Response (Most People's First Reaction)"What a rude person. They must be arrogant and selfish." You jump to a character (dispositional) explanation automatically.
What You Likely IgnoredThe student just got a call that a family member is in the hospital. They are in a complete panic. The situation explains the behavior perfectly, but you never knew about it.
The AsymmetryIf YOU bumped into someone while distracted, you would think "I was distracted, it was an accident" (situational). But you judge the other person's identical behavior as a character flaw. This asymmetry is the actor-observer effect layered on top of the FAE.
Reducing the ErrorAsk yourself: "What might the situation be?" This single habit dramatically reduces the FAE and tends to make people more empathetic and accurate in their social judgments.

Attitudes & Persuasion

Cognitive dissonance (Festinger) occurs when our behavior conflicts with our attitudes, creating psychological discomfort that motivates attitude change. The foot-in-the-door technique (small request first, then larger) and door-in-the-face technique (large request first, then smaller) exploit this. The elaboration likelihood model distinguishes central route (careful analysis of arguments) from peripheral route (superficial cues like attractiveness).

Prosocial Behavior & Aggression

The bystander effect (Darley & Latane) shows that the more bystanders present, the less likely any one person is to help, due to diffusion of responsibility and pluralistic ignorance. Altruism is selfless helping behavior. The frustration-aggression hypothesis states that frustration increases the likelihood of aggression.

Prejudice & Discrimination

Prejudice is an unjustifiable negative attitude toward a group. Discrimination is unjustifiable negative action toward a group. Stereotypes are generalized beliefs about a group. In-group bias favors one's own group. The just-world hypothesis leads people to believe others get what they deserve. Stereotype threat (Steele) occurs when awareness of a negative stereotype about one's group impairs performance.

Key Concept
Contact hypothesis (Allport): Prejudice can be reduced when groups interact under conditions of equal status, common goals, intergroup cooperation, and support from authorities.

Designing a Study: Correlation vs. Experiment

A researcher wants to know whether listening to music while studying improves exam scores. Let's design both a correlational study and an experiment and see why only one can prove causation.

Correlational ApproachSurvey 200 students: "Do you listen to music while studying?" Then compare their GPA. Result: music listeners have higher GPAs. Conclusion you CAN draw: music-listening and GPA are positively correlated.
Why Correlation Cannot Show Causation HereMaybe students who are more motivated (a third variable) both study with music AND get better grades. The music itself may have nothing to do with it. You cannot tell from correlation alone.
Experimental ApproachTake 100 students. Use random assignment to divide them into two groups. Group A studies with music; Group B studies in silence. Both groups take the same test. The only difference between groups is the IV (music vs. no music).
Why the Experiment CAN Show CausationRandom assignment distributes motivation, intelligence, and all other variables equally across both groups. If Group A scores higher, music must be the reason — because everything else was controlled.
